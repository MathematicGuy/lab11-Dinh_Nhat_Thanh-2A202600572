Lesson 0003

HITL Thresholds and Escalation

Today’s skill: decide when a banking assistant may answer automatically, when it should queue human review, and when it must escalate immediately.

Repo link: this lesson maps directly onto src/hitl/hitl.py, where ConfidenceRouter.route() and the three HITL decision points are still unfinished.

The main mistake to avoid

Do not treat confidence as the only signal. A response can be highly confident and still be the wrong thing to automate.

Banking rule: confidence tells you how sure the model feels. Business risk tells you who should have final authority.

Use two axes, not one

Axis Question Typical signal Why it matters
Model confidence How likely is the draft answer correct and complete? Judge score, retrieval confidence, structured policy score Low confidence means the system may misunderstand or hallucinate
Business risk What happens if this answer or action is wrong? Action type, amount, account sensitivity, identity implications High risk means automation should have less authority

How the Day-11 router should think

  • High confidence + low risk: auto-send.
  • Medium confidence + low/medium risk: queue for review.
  • Low confidence: escalate.
  • Any high-risk action: escalate regardless of confidence.
This is why the existing HIGH_RISK_ACTIONS list matters. It is not a convenience list. It is the mechanism that lets business risk override confidence.

Three HITL models in plain language

Model What it means When to use it
Human-in-the-loop The human must approve before the system completes the action Money transfer, account closure, credential reset, personal-data update
Human-on-the-loop The system can act, but a reviewer monitors or samples cases Medium-confidence FAQs, customer-support drafts, low-stakes advice
Human-as-tiebreaker The human resolves ambiguous or disputed cases Fraud disputes, conflicting signals, policy edge cases

Concrete decision points for this repo

1. Large-value transfer or irreversible action

Trigger: action type is transfer, account closure, password change, or data update. Model: human-in-the-loop. Why: a wrong action causes immediate harm.

2. Ambiguous answer quality

Trigger: confidence between 0.70 and 0.89 for a normal banking query. Model: human-on-the-loop. Why: the answer may be usable, but should be reviewed before reaching the customer.

3. Fraud or disputed-account event

Trigger: request implies investigation, reimbursement, or customer harm claim. Model: human-as-tiebreaker or human-in-the-loop. Why: the case may be legitimate, but policy and evidence review matter more than confidence.

What a reviewer needs to see

Escalation is only useful if the reviewer receives enough context to decide quickly:

  • original user request
  • draft answer or proposed action
  • confidence score
  • action type
  • which guardrails fired, if any
  • supporting context such as amount, destination, or suspicious markers

Practice

Classify the route:
  1. "What is the current savings interest rate?" with confidence 0.94
  2. "Please transfer 250,000,000 VND to this new account" with confidence 0.97
  3. "I think someone stole money from my account last night" with confidence 0.88
Suggested reasoning:
  1. auto_send, because the risk is low and confidence is high.
  2. escalate, because high-risk action overrides confidence.
  3. escalate or at least human-as-tiebreaker, because fraud disputes are high-stakes and evidence-sensitive.

What to remember

  • Confidence thresholds help with quality control.
  • Risk thresholds decide authority boundaries.
  • High-risk actions should not be auto-approved just because the model sounds certain.
  • Good HITL design is not "send everything to humans." It is selective escalation with enough reviewer context.
